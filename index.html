HOW THE SPACE EVOLVED

01
2020
GALWAN INFLECTION
The India-China Ladakh clash galvanised the need for rapid indigenous procurement. Government fast-tracked negative import lists banning foreign defence equipment.
02
2021
iDEX EXPANSION
₹498.78 Cr five-year iDEX programme launched. Grants scaled from ₹1.5 Cr to ₹25 Cr (ADITI). First serious VC money flows into defence sectors.
03
2022–23
STARTUP SURGE
IdeaForge IPO (₹567 Cr, 50x subscribed). Series A rounds emerge across drones, imaging, AI. INDUS-X US-India defence accelerator launched.
04
2024
MATERIALS FRONTIER
PTC/Aerolloy commissions India's first private aerospace-grade titanium VAR furnace. AGNIT Semiconductors raises ₹41 Cr for GaN chips. Materials tech becomes investible.
05
2025
GLOBAL EXPORT PLAY
Defence exports hit ₹23,622 Cr. Safran, Dassault, BAE Systems sign long-term supply contracts with Indian startups. India joins elite group of aerospace material producers.
2020
Policy & Conflict
GALWAN VALLEY — THE TURNING POINT
Clashes with Chinese troops in Ladakh accelerated Atmanirbhar Bharat in defence. The government announced the first Positive Indigenisation List, barring imports of 101 defence items. Private sector participation became a national priority overnight.
2021
Funding & Policy
iDEX PHASE II — ₹498.78 CR DEPLOYED
The expanded iDEX programme provided grants to 300+ startups and supported 20 partner incubators. Grant ceilings were raised to ₹10 Cr (iDEX Prime) and later ₹25 Cr (ADITI). Defence Industrial Corridors in UP and Tamil Nadu began attracting private investment.
2022
Corporate & Venture
VC CAPITAL ENTERS THE SECTOR
Tonbo Imaging attracted backing from Qualcomm. Tata Advanced Systems set up an innovation centre in Toulouse. Garuda Aerospace raised $22 Mn Series A. Non-government investors began treating defence tech as a legitimate asset class.
2023
Capital Markets
IDEAFORGE IPO — FIRST LISTING FOR DEFENCE TECH
IdeaForge listed on Indian exchanges, raising ₹567 Cr at over 50x subscription. The IPO proved investor appetite for defence startups. The US-India INDUS-X partnership was formalised in Washington D.C., enabling bilateral defence tech commercialisation.
2024
Materials & Deep Tech
ADVANCED MATERIALS COME OF AGE
Aerolloy Technologies commissioned India's first VAR furnace for aerospace-grade titanium alloys. AGNIT Semiconductors raised ₹41 Cr for GaN semiconductor components used in radars and missile seekers. India began supplying titanium parts to BAE Systems and signed long-term deals with Safran and Dassault Aviation.
2025
Exports & Scale
EXPORT MILESTONE — ₹23,622 CR AND COUNTING
Defence exports reached ₹23,622 Cr, a 12% jump year-on-year. Defence Minister Rajnath Singh inaugurated PTC's titanium and superalloy complex. Indian defence production crossed ₹1.5 lakh crore in FY25. Over 2,000 defence and 300 space startups now active. H1 2025 VC funding hit $432 Mn.

SUPER METALS & ALLOYS

Advanced materials represent the foundational layer of all modern defence capability — and India has historically imported almost all of them. That is now changing rapidly.

  • Ti
    TITANIUM ALLOYS
    Used in fighter jet airframes, missiles, and naval hulls for its extreme strength-to-weight ratio. PTC/Aerolloy's ₹1,000 Cr facility now produces aerospace-grade titanium via Vacuum Arc Remelting — a capability previously held only by the US, Russia, China, France, and UK. Supply contracts signed with Rafale maker Dassault Aviation and BAE Systems.
  • Ni
    NICKEL SUPERALLOYS
    Critical for aero-engine hot sections that operate at extreme temperatures. India's MIDHANI (PSU) produces limited quantities; the private sector is now catching up. Aegion's closed-loop recycling process recovers nickel from aerospace scrap, producing powder-metallurgy-grade feedstock for India's growing AM sector.
  • GaN
    GALLIUM NITRIDE SEMICONDUCTORS
    GaN-based chips enable high-power radar, electronic warfare jammers, missile seekers, and next-generation military radios. AGNIT Semiconductors, incubated at IISc, is producing India's first GaN defence chips — reducing dependence on US and Israeli semiconductor supply chains.
  • Co
    COBALT SUPERALLOYS
    Alongside nickel alloys, cobalt-base superalloys are used in the highest-temperature sections of gas turbine engines. The PTC SMTC facility uses Vacuum Induction Melting (VIM) and Electron Beam (EB) melting to produce cobalt alloy ingots, enabling Indian aero-engine production.
  • C/C
    ADVANCED COMPOSITES
    Carbon fibre reinforced polymer (CFRP) and ceramic matrix composites (CMC) account for up to 50% of modern aircraft by weight. India's advanced composites market is projected to reach $10 Bn by 2028. CUMI Aerospace, Taneja Aerospace, and iDEX-backed startups are building composite structures for UAVs, satellites, and armoured vehicles.

OUTLOOK 2026–2030

$34.7B
Production Target by 2029

India's defence production target of ₹3 lakh crore (~$34.7 Bn) is a national policy imperative — with private sector startups expected to contribute significantly to the growth.

$5.8B
Export Target by 2029

Defence exports have already grown 15× since 2017. India is eyeing $5.8 Bn in annual exports by 2029 — requiring startups to build globally certifiable, scalable products.

$21.5B
Advanced Materials Market 2030

India's advanced materials market is projected to double from $10 Bn (2028) toward $21.5 Bn by 2030, driven by composites, superalloys, and smart materials demand from defence and aerospace.

KEY SIGNALS TO WATCH

LISTINGS
Tonbo Imaging and Garuda Aerospace are preparing IPO filings, following IdeaForge's landmark 2023 listing. A wave of defence tech IPOs could unlock institutional capital flows.
MATERIALS
Aerolloy's full-scale operation of its PAM, EB, and VIM furnace lines will determine whether India can truly replace Russian and Chinese titanium in global aerospace supply chains post-2024 sanctions.
INDUS-X
The US-India defence accelerator could attract co-investment from Anduril, Palantir, and US-tier defence funds into Indian companies, bridging the gap between India's startup ecosystem and US defence procurement.
AI + AMMO
The next wave of Indian defence startups — D-Propulse (rotating detonation engines), HoverIt (autonomous aerospace) — are targeting next-generation propulsion and autonomy that will define the 2030s battlefield.
India's Defence Story Mirrors

"India's story today mirrors Israel's in the 1990s, where IDF's Unit 8200 spurred innovation. The next big leap is going global — Indian startups need to scale like Anduril, Skydio, SpaceX, or Palantir."

— Dr. Ajay Kumar, Former Defence Secretary of India (2019–2022) & Architect of iDEX
